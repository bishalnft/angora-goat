Angora Goat

About Angora Goat

The Angora or Ankara is a Turkish breed of domesticated goat. It produces the lustrous fibre known as mohair. It is widespread in many countries of the world. Many breeds derive from it, among them the Indian Mohair, the Soviet Mohair, the Angora-Don of the Russian Federation and the Pygora in the United States.

Habitat:

Angora goats thrive in mostly semi-arid regions with dry, hot summers and cold winters. They originated in Asia Minor and were first successfully exported to other countries beginning in the mid-19th century.

Diet:

Roughage is important for proper health and rumen function, so good quality pasture or hay is the foundation of any solid Angora feeding program. Goats are good foragers and eat a wide variety of weeds, woody plants, shrubs, briars and grasses.

Each Angora goat is shaved twice yearly to collect the fiber.

Why are Angora Goat's Important?

Angora goats are famous for their fleece. They have thick and curly coat similar to sheep coat. Angora goat breed is the only one which can produce a mohair (a sort of lustrous fiber) and this is being used in garment industry to manufacture sweaters. One Angora goat can produce 5 to 6 kg of mohair per year.

Why are Angora Goat's Endangered?

They are listed as near threatened by IUCN and in cites appendix i. They are native to the Palearctic. They are solitary, diurnal herbivores. Individuals are known to live for 168 months and can grow to 1590 mm.
